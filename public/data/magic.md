# MAGIC Framework
**Managed AI Governance & Implementation Controls**
_Run with the GSO Loop: Govern. Secure. Operate._

---

## What this is

The **MAGIC Framework** is an MSP-native, controls-first framework for enabling AI across customer environments in a way that is **repeatable, governable, and monetizable**.

MAGIC is not a theory of AI. It is not a list of tools. It is a **control system** designed for Managed Service Providers who are responsible for operating AI across dozens or hundreds of tenants, each with different risk profiles, data realities, and business goals.

If CIS Controls are how MSPs made cybersecurity operational, **MAGIC is how MSPs make AI operational**.

---

## The Core Model: GSO

MAGIC is executed using a simple, durable operating loop:

### **Govern. Secure. Operate.**

This triad is intentional. It mirrors how MSPs already deliver mature services and avoids the two most common AI failure modes: unmanaged experimentation and over-engineered policy theater.

### Govern
Establish clear ownership, intent, and boundaries before AI spreads.

Governance in MAGIC is practical, not academic. It answers:
- Who owns AI decisions in this tenant?
- What outcomes are we trying to produce?
- Which users, tools, and use cases are in scope right now?
- How do changes get approved, reviewed, or rolled back?

Governance happens **before** broad enablement and continues throughout the lifecycle.

### Secure
Reduce risk by making AI respect identity, data, and environment boundaries.

Security in MAGIC assumes AI amplifies whatever already exists. Good or bad.

Security controls focus on:
- Identity and access hygiene before AI access is granted
- Data scope, classification, and oversharing cleanup
- Technical guardrails that enforce policy, not just document it
- Auditability and evidence, not assumptions

Security is not "lock everything down." It is **intentional containment**.

### Operate
Run AI like a production service, not a pilot that never ends.

Operation is where most AI programs fail. MAGIC treats AI as something that must be:
- Deployed in stages
- Supported by service desks
- Observed, measured, and improved
- Rolled back when it misbehaves

If you cannot operate it, you should not enable it.

## What makes it into the MAGIC Framework

MAGIC is deliberately strict about what qualifies as a control.

A control belongs in the framework if it meets **all** of the following criteria:

### 1. It is operational, not conceptual
Controls must describe something an MSP or customer can actually do, configure, review, or produce evidence for.

Good:
- "Named executive sponsor assigned with approval authority"
- "Copilot access scoped to Entra ID pilot group"
- "Restricted SharePoint search enabled with approved site list"

Not good:
- "Have an AI strategy"
- "Ensure responsible AI usage"
- "Promote innovation"

### 2. It has a clear owner
Every control must be owned by a real role:
- Customer executive
- MSP vCIO
- Security lead
- Platform owner
- Service delivery manager

If no one owns it, it does not exist.

### 3. It can produce evidence
If a control cannot be validated with evidence, it does not belong.

Evidence might include:
- A policy document
- A configuration export
- A dashboard
- A meeting cadence
- A decision log
- A checklist with sign-off

MAGIC assumes audits, QBRs, and customer scrutiny are normal.

### 4. It scales across tenants
Controls must be repeatable across customers with different sizes and industries.

If a control only works for:
- One-off consulting
- Custom development
- Hero engineers

It does not belong in the framework.

### 5. It aligns to the GSO lifecycle
Every control must clearly support **Govern**, **Secure**, or **Operate**.

If it does not strengthen one of those motions, it is noise.

## What explicitly does NOT belong in the framework

This matters as much as what is included.

### 1. Tool comparisons and vendor rankings
MAGIC is tool-aware, not tool-biased.

The framework may reference **classes of tooling** or **Microsoft-native controls**, but it does not:
- Rank vendors
- Recommend "best AI tools"
- Chase features or hype cycles

Tools change faster than controls.

### 2. Custom application development guidance
MAGIC is not a software engineering framework.

It intentionally avoids:
- How to build models
- How to tune prompts for edge cases
- How to write custom AI applications

Those belong in product teams, not MSP control planes.

### 3. Ethical philosophy without enforcement
MAGIC is not an ethics manifesto.

Ethics only appear when they are enforceable through:
- Policy
- Approval workflows
- Access restrictions
- Audit and review

If it cannot be enforced, it is not a control.

### 4. One-time assessments with no operating model
MAGIC rejects "check-the-box" AI readiness assessments.

Every control assumes:
- Ongoing cadence
- Review and drift
- Expansion or rollback decisions

If a control only exists at onboarding, it is incomplete.

### 5. End-user productivity tips
MAGIC is not a prompt library or training course.

While training and enablement are governed, the framework does not include:
- Prompt examples
- Workflow hacks
- "Top 10 Copilot tips"

Those belong in enablement programs, not governance frameworks.

## Why MSPs need MAGIC specifically

AI changes the MSP risk equation.

An MSP does not enable AI once. It enables AI:
- Across many customers
- Across many tools
- Across many regulatory environments
- Across many business models

That creates **compound risk** and **compound opportunity**.

MAGIC exists to:
- Turn AI from a consulting motion into a managed service
- Prevent shadow AI from becoming the default operating model
- Give MSPs a defensible, repeatable way to say "yes" to AI without losing control
- Create a shared language between MSPs, customers, security teams, and executives

---

## How MAGIC is meant to be used

MAGIC is designed to be:
- Open and inspectable
- Implemented incrementally
- Operated continuously

Typical usage patterns include:
- MSP internal standard for AI delivery
- Customer-facing readiness and maturity scoring
- QBR structure for AI adoption and risk
- Control plane input for platforms like Lemhi Compass

MAGIC is the **map**.
Your platform is the **vehicle**.
